Learn the what, why
and how of experimentation.

Before you build an experiment, understand why it matters. This page covers the fundamentals — in plain English, no PhD required.

What

What is experimentation?

An experiment is a controlled way to test whether a change causes an improvement — not just correlates with one. That distinction matters more than almost anything else in data science.

Here's the problem with just looking at data: if sales go up after you launch a new feature, did the feature cause it? Or was it a holiday? A competitor going down? Seasonal trends? Without an experiment, you genuinely cannot tell.

An experiment solves this by randomly splitting your users into two groups — one sees the change (treatment), one doesn't (control). Because the split is random, both groups are essentially identical. Any difference in outcome between them can only be explained by the change you made. That's causation, not correlation.

The math then tells you whether the difference you observed is real or just luck. That's what statistical significance means — not that the result is important, but that it's unlikely to have happened by chance.

Why

Why do companies run experiments?

Because most ideas fail. Not 10% of ideas — most. At Amazon, Microsoft, and Google, internal studies suggest that only 10–30% of experiments show a positive effect. The rest are neutral or harmful.

That sounds discouraging. It's actually the point. Experimentation is how you find the 20% that works before you ship it to everyone. Without experiments, companies ship everything and hope. With experiments, they only ship what they know works.

The compounding effect is enormous. Amazon runs over 10,000 experiments a year. Each one that works makes their product incrementally better. Over a decade, that's thousands of validated improvements — none of which would have been possible without a system for being right more often than they're wrong.

For you personally: the ability to design, run, and interpret experiments is one of the highest-leverage skills in modern data science. It's what separates analysts who describe what happened from scientists who determine what caused it.

How

How does an experiment work?

01
Form a hypothesis
State clearly what you believe will happen and why. "If we show users X, then metric Y will improve, because Z." Vague hypotheses produce uninterpretable results.
→ Read: Trustworthy Online Controlled Experiments — experimentguide.com
02
Pick the right method
Can you randomize? Do you have pre-treatment data? How large is your sample? The answers determine whether you run an A/B test, a DiD, a Synthetic Control, or something else.
→ Use Tool 01 to find your method
03
Calculate how many users you need
Running a test too short means you might miss a real effect. Running it too long wastes time and money. Sample size calculation tells you exactly how many users you need before you start.
→ Use Tool 02 to calculate sample size
04
Validate randomization before trusting results
Sample Ratio Mismatch (SRM) is when your actual split doesn't match your intended split. It silently corrupts results and is more common than you'd think. Always check before analyzing.
→ Use Tool 03 to check your split
05
Analyze results
Did the change work? By how much? Is the result statistically significant? What's the confidence interval? These questions have precise answers — not vibes.
→ Use Tool 04 to check significance
06
Present your findings
A great experiment with a bad readout doesn't get shipped. Your job isn't done when the analysis is done — it's done when the decision is made. Clear communication is part of the science.
